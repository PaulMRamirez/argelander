Acquisition Geometry Atlas

Twenty-one ways instruments actually touch the ground, past, current, and upcoming, animated on a neutral map, then six ways to paint the same coverage. Geometry and treatment are independent decisions; the target is a renderer-agnostic component for MMGIS, with Leaflet and three.js adapters. This page exists so those decisions get made deliberately.

Design exploration, not the engine. This atlas is hand-authored to decide how each geometry and treatment should read; it is not the Argelander engine's output. The live Leaflet demo holds the true current visualization state, rendered by the engine from real states.

A · Geometry: how the instrument touches the ground

Every tile is the same orbit clock. Repeat passes shift cross-track, as they do on orbit, so persistence shows how coverage accrues. The bright cyan element is the thing the instrument is doing right now; teal is what it has already committed to disk.

B · Treatment: how the same coverage is painted

Six canvases, one clock, identical crossing passes (an ascending and a descending pushbroom). Only the paint differs. Each treatment answers a different operator question, and that question, not taste, is how you pick.

The decisions on the table

Decision A is geometry fidelity. Envelope ribbons are cheap and read instantly; beam-level beads, burst quilts, and arc stacks are honest to the sampling but cost geometry generation and clutter at small scale. A sane default: envelope at low zoom, mechanism detail past a zoom threshold, always with the instantaneous aperture drawn live.

Decision B is treatment, chosen per question the layer answers: boundaries (outline), coverage and look-count (flat fill), what is being sensed now (leading edge plus fading trail), which instrument (mechanism texture), sample quality (edge gradient), or acquisition time (hue ramp). They compose freely with any geometry above.

One rule worth keeping global: reserve hue for state (cyan now, teal accumulated, amber planned) and let texture carry instrument identity. That keeps a ten-layer footprint display legible instead of rainbow soup.

Decision C is the integration surface. The engine stays renderer-agnostic: instrument models and ephemerides in, time-tagged footprint strips out. Adapters bind it to MMGIS: a Leaflet canvas-overlay layer first (the mature 2D Map), a three.js layer for the Globe second, both fed by the same strips. Planned-versus-executed state (amber to teal) drops directly onto plan-aware map state, and coverage accrual becomes a computed layer in the SPEC-02 sense. The companion survey document carries the full mission catalog, the requirements draft, and the phased plan. The same core is written to be hosted beyond MMGIS: Bessel as the state provider on the way in, strips typed as analysis products on the way out, Cosmolabe drawing them as geometry drapes; standalone first, convergence by contract. Component proper name: Argelander, for Bessel's pupil whose Bonner Durchmusterung surveyed the sky in drift-scan zones; AGE stays the functional identity.
